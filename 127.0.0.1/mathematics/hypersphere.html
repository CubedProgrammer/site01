Hypersphere

Spheres can be generalized to higher dimensions, defined as the shape that has the lowest surface area to volume ratio. The hyper volume of a sphere in higher dimensions is given by this formula.

raπa/2/Γ(a/2+1)

Where a is the dimension and r is the radius.

This page is not interested in the volume though, we are interested in packing hyperspheres inside hypercubes.

Starting with the square and the circle, imagine a square of length 4. Put 4 circles of radius 1 in each corner. There is some room left in the middle, let us put another circle there so it touches the four circles.

What is the radius of the inner circle?

Imagine a cube of length 4, put 8 spheres of radius 1 in each corner, squeeze in the inner sphere again. This time, what is its radius? Greater than the inner circle in two dimensions?

Imagine a hypercube of length 4, put 16 hypersphers of radius 1 in each corner, and put in the inner hypersphere as usual. What is the radius of the inner hypersphere?

The radius will always be the distance from the center of a corner hypersphere to the center of the cube, minus 1 for the corner hypersphers of radius 1. The distance to the center of the cube is the square root of the dimension.

The radius of the inner hypersphere grows as the number of dimensions increase, at 4 dimensions, it is already the same size as the corner hyperspheres. Let us see what happens in even higher dimensions.

Dimension Radius

The radius of the inner hypersphere in 10 dimensions is most interesting, with a radius greater than 2, this means the inner hypersphere goes outside of the cube of length 4 that bounds the corner hyperspheres.

We cannot imagine it in three dimensions, but in such high dimensions the corner of a hypercube is so far away from its center that this can happen.
